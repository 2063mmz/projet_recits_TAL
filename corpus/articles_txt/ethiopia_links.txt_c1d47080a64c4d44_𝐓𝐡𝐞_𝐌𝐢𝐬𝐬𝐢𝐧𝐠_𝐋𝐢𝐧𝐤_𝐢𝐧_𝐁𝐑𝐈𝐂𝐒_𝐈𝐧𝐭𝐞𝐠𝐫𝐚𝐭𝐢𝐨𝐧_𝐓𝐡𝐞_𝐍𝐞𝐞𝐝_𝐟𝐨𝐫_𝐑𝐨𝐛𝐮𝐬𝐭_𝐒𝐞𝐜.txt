Introduction
The BRICS groupingâ€”comprising Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africaâ€”was founded on three major tenets: political and security, economic and financial, and cultural and people-to-people cooperation. Despite its vast scope of interests and goals, BRICS is still seen as a forum for economic and developmental partnership, rather than a comprehensive political or security alliance (Mdlokovana, 2025).
In BRICS, the level of cooperation on security is rather
low and tends to be bilateral.
There is no collective mechanism established among the member countries on a formal level and as well the platform lacks shared vision on security threats (Cutrera, 2020). In fact, BRICS countries have different views on security. Whereas some member countries tend to perceive it as a military risk; others as sovereignty, or non-intervention, economic security, or some other threats related to cyberspace. These differences make a unified strategy unlikely.
This means that the BRICS platform lacks the ability to address to common security issues, resolve conflicts, and assure solidarity to its member states in case of external threats. This is even more reinforced by the varied and, at some instances, divergent geopolitical agendas of its member states. This is going to be even more complex considering that there are new BRICS member states including Egypt
Ethiopia, Iran, United Arab Emirates, Indonesia, and other nine partner Countries included in the platform (
Makhija
, 2025).
There are many instances wherein
the
BRICS members have taken
contradicting stances within regional conflicts.
A case in point includes Chinaâ€™s strategic partnership with Pakistan which conflicts with Indiaâ€™s national security imperatives (Noor, 2014). This exemplifies that a structural problem may be created within BRICS if a security mechanism is not in place, and hence this may widen gaps in cooperation and hinder deeper integration.
The Need for Security
Mechanisms
Even if the BRICS members share a common vision of transforming global governance and achieving a more multipolar international system, they diverge on their security strategy priorities, threat perceptions, and regional roles which make it challenging to consolidate the bloc (
De Carvalho
, 2025). Every member operates in a different geological setting, thereby entertaining separate security threats, such as regional conflicts, border disputes, terrorism, cyber, and economic coercion, among others, which might hamper coordination of security policies and as a result weaken collective threats actions.
Under such a situation, it is imperative for BRICS to develop appropriate security mechanisms to handle the divergence within the bloc and foster more security among each other, build mutual trust and avoid security deviations that might hamper cooperation in this regard. Towards a Strong Security Framework
Of more than 180 BRICS cooperation mechanisms, very few actually operate within the security arena. These include: the BRICS National Security Advisors Meeting (endorsed at the II BRICS Summitâ€”Brasilia Joint Statement, 2010); the BRICS Anti-Money Laundering and Countering the Financing of Terrorism Council (endorsed at the VII BRICS Summitâ€”Ufa Declaration, 2015); the BRICS Working Group on Security in the Use of ICTs (endorsed at the VII BRICS Summitâ€”Ufa Declaration, 2015); and the BRICS Working Group on Counter-Terrorism (endorsed at the VIII BRICS Summitâ€”Goa Declaration, 2016).
Even with the mechanisms above, it can be noted that their scope is dominantly placed within the counter-terrorism and
cyber security, rather than encompassing broader or collective security concerns in real terms.
If BRICS is to emerge as a strategic coalition in its true form, it has to move beyond economic coordination and adopt
more comprehensive strategic approach to security. The comprehensive security framework would give rise to an opportunity where conflicts can be dealt with on a collaborative basis and would also facilitate increased transparency among its members. Adopting a collective approach would also increase its stature on the international scene and would give BRICS a much more significant opportunity to play its part in international security.
Preventing Negative
Repercussions
Without binding security cooperation measures in place, member states remain free to support external actors outside the bloc that may be at odds with fellow BRICS members. Such actions set troubling precedents that can be repeated and amplified. In cases where one BRICS member finds itself supporting an ally of an adversary of another, such incidents create
conflicts
of interest between member states, eroding trust and undermining strategic cohesion within the bloc. Ideally, a structured security strategy would help limit these contradictions by establishing
clear
and expectations
reducing the likelihood of members taking actions that harm each otherâ€™s interests.
Avoiding Support for Opponents of Member States
Being such a long-term cooperative framework, BRICS faces threats if its member states support organizations or countries that can potentially threaten other member states. If such patterns continue, there is every possibility that its relevance and credibility can diminish. For development of its security strategies, there should provisions that can deter member states from providing political, financial, or military support to any actor that can threaten other BRICS member countries. In such a way the bloc can better maintain unity and strengthen its collective voice in global affairs by promoting internal solidarity.
Conclusion
Indeed, it can be argued that to date, the BRICS grouping has not yet materialized or can be said â€œfailed toâ€ carve out a comprehensive security strategy, which is partly owing to the divers , sometimes conflicting, geopolitical interests of its member states. that belong to the same alliance umbrella . Thus, it is recommended that in order to overcome these challenges, and strengthen the blocâ€™s strategic relevance, BRICS ought to make efforts to collectively carve out a shared security framework that includes common principles, conflict-prevention, improved intelligence sharing, and informal norms to
desist from taking the side of those who might be considered hostile to any member state could be incorporated. Additionally, it would also be of paramount
importance to include initiatives such as structured security dialogues and
peacekeeping cooperation to deepen trust and help transform trust and help BRICS from a primarily economic partnership to a more coherent and influential global coalition.
